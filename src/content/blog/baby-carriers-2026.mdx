# Baby Carriers That Actually Span Newborn to Toddler (2026)

With two kids close in age, a carrier earns its keep differently than it does in a single-kid household — you might need it for the newborn while the toddler walks, or occasionally for the toddler while the newborn rides in a stroller, sometimes both at once. Carriers that need a separate infant insert add a step (and a thing to misplace) you don't have time for. Here's what's worth a look as of mid-2026.

## Quick picks

- **Best no-insert option from birth:** Ergobaby Embrace (7–25 lbs)
- **Best for growing into toddlerhood, including forward-facing:** Ergobaby Omni 360
- **Best adjustable-panel design (no insert, ever):** Baby Tula Free-to-Grow / Explore
- **Best budget pick with insert included:** Infantino Flip 4-in-1
- **Best for very new newborns specifically:** Contours Journey (from 8 lbs, no insert)

## Ergobaby Embrace

Built specifically for the newborn stage without requiring a separate insert — the soft knit fabric itself provides the support a structured carrier usually achieves with an add-on piece. It fits roughly 7–25 lbs and offers an inward-facing newborn position. The trade-off is that it's a newborn-stage carrier specifically, not a from-birth-to-preschool system — most parents pair it with a second carrier once their baby outgrows it, which is worth budgeting for if you're choosing this one.

## Ergobaby Omni 360

The more versatile long-haul option: four carry positions including forward-facing (useful once a baby is old enough to want to look outward, which often coincides with exactly the stage your toddler is still small enough to also want carrying sometimes), and no insert required at any stage. This is the carrier most likely to still be useful well into toddlerhood, which matters if you want one carrier doing double duty across both your kids' carrying years rather than buying separate carriers for each child's current stage.

## Baby Tula Free-to-Grow / Explore

Tula's adjustable body panel is designed to eliminate the insert question entirely — the panel itself adjusts to fit a newborn's smaller frame and grows with the child, rather than relying on a removable padded insert that needs to come in and out. Parents consistently mention the adjustment system as fast enough to use without fully removing the baby, which matters in exactly the moment you're also managing a toddler.

## Infantino Flip 4-in-1

The realistic budget pick — it includes an infant insert in the box rather than needing it purchased separately, supports roughly 8–32 lbs, and offers multiple carry positions. It won't have the refined feel of the premium options, but it covers the fundamentals at a price point that makes sense if you're also budgeting for a second kid's worth of everything else right now.

## Contours Journey

Worth a specific mention because it's rated from 8 lbs with no insert needed, and offers five carry positions including back-carry — useful once your toddler is the one wanting a ride and your arms are already full of the newborn.

## The tandem-wearing question

If you ever need to carry both kids at once — a real scenario in the early months when neither is independently mobile or both need comfort simultaneously — the realistic setup is a front carrier for the newborn (something like the Embrace or Omni 360, positioned high on the chest) paired with a back-carry option for the toddler once they're old enough for back-carrying, generally once they have solid head and neck control and can sit unassisted. This isn't a single-product solution — it's two carriers used together — and it's worth practicing the combination at home, briefly and with a second adult nearby the first few times, before relying on it out and about.
