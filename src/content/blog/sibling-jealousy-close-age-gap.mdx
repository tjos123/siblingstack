A toddler who's barely settled into being the only child is suddenly sharing your attention with a newborn — and unlike a bigger age gap, where the older kid might be old enough to feel some pride or independence about it, a small age gap often means your toddler is still developmentally very attached to you, with limited language to express what they're feeling.

## What it actually looks like at this age

With a smaller age gap, sibling jealousy more often shows up as **regression** than as outright aggression — wanting a bottle again, asking to be carried, sudden potty training setbacks, or wanting to be fed the way they see the baby being fed. It can also show up as clinginess that looks like the opposite of jealousy: a toddler who suddenly won't let you out of their sight isn't necessarily being naughty, they're often anxious about whether your attention is still reliably theirs.

Genuine aggression toward the baby does happen too, and it's common enough not to be alarming on its own — but it's worth taking seriously enough to never leave a toddler unsupervised with the newborn during this adjustment period.

## What tends to help

**Name what you notice, simply, without over-explaining.** "You wanted to be picked up first" or "it's hard to wait when you want me" gives a toddler language for what they're feeling. A short, accurate label is often more effective than a long reassurance.

**Give the toddler a real job, not a token one.** "Can you pick which blanket the baby uses?" gives a small amount of genuine involvement, which tends to land better than purely symbolic gestures.

**Protect a small, predictable piece of one-on-one time, even five or ten minutes.** Consistency matters more than duration — a short window your toddler can reliably count on tends to reduce anxiety-driven behaviour more than occasional longer stretches that happen unpredictably.

**Don't force affection toward the baby.** "Go give the baby a kiss" when a toddler clearly isn't in the mood can build resentment rather than bonding. Let warmth toward the sibling develop on the toddler's own timeline.

**Expect it to ebb and flow, not resolve in a straight line.** A toddler might seem completely fine for a week and then regress again — often around a new developmental stage of their own, rather than because of anything that changed with the baby.

## When to bring in extra support

Most sibling adjustment difficulty at this age is developmentally normal and settles within a few months. If aggression toward the baby is frequent, intense, or not easing over time, or if you're finding it hard to manage your own stress around the situation, it's worth talking to your paediatrician. They can help distinguish ordinary adjustment from something that warrants more support.
