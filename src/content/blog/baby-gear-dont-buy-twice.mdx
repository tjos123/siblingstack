When two kids are close enough in age that their needs overlap, it's tempting to assume you need two of everything, just offset by a few months. In practice, a fair amount of baby gear can be shared, staggered, or skipped entirely the second time around — you just need to know which items actually require duplicates and which don't.

## Items you genuinely need two of

**Car seats**, for safety and legal reasons covered in more detail in [our car seat guide](/blog/car-seat-two-different-sizes) — there's no getting around this one.

**Sleep surfaces, if both kids are sleeping at the same time.** A bassinet or crib can't be in two places handling two kids simultaneously, so if your toddler still naps in a crib and your newborn needs one too, you need both available at once, even if one is borrowed, secondhand, or a pack-and-play standing in for a second crib temporarily.

**High chairs, once both kids are eating solids around the table together.** This one sneaks up on people — it doesn't feel urgent until the day you actually have two kids eating dinner at the same time and only one chair.

## Items you can stagger instead of duplicating

**Bouncers, swings, and play mats.** These are rarely needed by both kids at the exact same moment — your toddler has usually outgrown the bouncer stage by the time the newborn needs it, which means hand-me-down timing often works in your favor here rather than against it.

**Bath tubs/inserts.** Bathing two kids at once is possible but most families end up staggering bath time anyway once the newborn stage passes, which means one infant tub insert is usually enough even with two kids close in age.

**Baby carriers**, if you and a partner or co-caregiver aren't both wearing a baby at the same time. One quality carrier, used by whoever's on baby duty at a given moment, covers most situations — a second is only worth it if both caregivers are regularly carrying simultaneously.

## Items where buying once but choosing carefully pays off twice

**A convertible/expandable crib**, if you're staggering rather than running two cribs in parallel. Choosing one crib that converts as the child grows, rather than buying a bassinet-then-crib-then-toddler-bed sequence, can mean you only fully furnish a sleep setup once and just shift which kid uses which space as ages change.

**Clothing in durable, gender-neutral basics**, if you might have more kids or don't know future family plans — durable basics (not the delicate going-home outfit, the everyday onesies) survive a second or third kid's wear far better than fast-fashion baby clothing does, making the per-use cost much lower even though the upfront item is the same one.

## Where people overspend without realizing it

The most common overspend in this situation isn't any single big item — it's a string of small "just in case" purchases (a second diaper bag, a second set of bottles, a second baby monitor) bought reflexively because "we have two kids now," when the actual daily routine only ever needs one of that item in use at a time. Before buying a second of anything, it's worth asking a simple question: will both kids need this at the literal same moment, regularly? If the honest answer is no, staggering or sharing usually works fine.
